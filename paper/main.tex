\begin{abstract}
We mechanize a corrected equivalence between two standard characterizations of
d-separation in Lean~4, exhibit a concrete counterexample to the unrestricted
statement, and build a witness extractor from graph reachability.

Standard textbook presentations in Pearl's \emph{Causality}~\cite{pearl2009causality},
Bishop's \emph{Pattern Recognition and Machine Learning}~\cite{bishop2006prml},
and Koller and Friedman's \emph{Probabilistic Graphical Models}~\cite{koller2009probabilistic}
present trail blocking and moralized ancestral-graph separation in the standard
pairwise-disjoint query form.  We machine-check a concrete counterexample to the
unrestricted statement and prove the corrected equivalence under
$\text{DisjointSets}\,X\,Y\,Z$.

The forward direction compiles any active trail through a Bayes-ball state
machine into a compressed moral-graph walk.  The reverse direction
reconstructs an explicit active-trail witness from moral-graph reachability,
including a proved bad-collider normalization (\lean{route_improves_of_bad})
that decreases a well-founded measure.  Together, both directions yield
\lean{dSeparated_iff_dSeparates} on the pairwise-disjoint domain.

The graph result supports a finite Shannon-leakage bound.  We bridge to
operational QIF through a two-stage factorisation split: an upper computable
topological layer producing \lean{decide}-able Markov-blanket and local-Markov
boundary conditions, and a lower typed adapter that turns those node-set
premises into the probabilistic conditional-independence predicate
\lean{condMarkov}.  Composing the split with a dual KL witness yields an upper
bound for information leakage from structural d-separation to quantitative
capacity, conditional on an external factorization hypothesis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Formalizing both standard characterizations of d-separation in
Lean~4's dependent type system yields a mechanized counterexample: the
unrestricted equivalence between Pearl's trail-blocking predicate and
Lauritzen's moralized ancestral-graph separation is \emph{false}.  When an
endpoint node lies in the conditioning set~$Z$, the moral-graph construction
deletes that endpoint while the trail predicate still admits the one-edge trail
that has no internal triple.

The counterexample (Section~\ref{sec:counterexample}) lives on a
three-node chain and exhibits the disagreement on a single endpoint
configuration.

We mechanize this discrepancy and its repair, treating the two
characterizations not as interchangeable definitions but as a checked
equivalence between two semantic layers:
\begin{itemize}
  \item an \textbf{operational semantics} of active trails, with
    \lean{DisjointSets} as the disjointness side condition on the
    conditioning set;
  \item a \textbf{static analysis} of moralized-graph reachability, with the
    collider \lean{MAGWalk.jump} as the compression step;
  \item a \textbf{witness extractor} from static reachability back to an
    operational witness, i.e.\ a Bayes-ball
    \lean{StaticRoute}~$\to$~\lean{OpenTrace}~$\to$~\lean{ActiveRoute}
    pipeline.
\end{itemize}
The forward direction is a soundness result: it compiles an operational trace
into static reachability evidence.  The reverse direction reconstructs a
concrete execution witness from static reachability alone.  The counterexample
shows that the two languages are not equivalent without the disjointness
side condition.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item We machine-check a counterexample.  It shows that unrestricted trail
    blocking and moral-graph separation are not equivalent; the repair is
    $\text{DisjointSets}\,X\,Y\,Z$.
  \item We formalize both d-separation characterizations in Lean~4 as a
    three-layer stack: graph syntax, certified intermediate representations
    (\lean{MAGWalk}, \lean{StaticRoute}, \lean{OpenTrace}, \lean{ActiveRoute}),
    and witness-extraction pipelines.
  \item We mechanize the forward compiler
    (Section~\ref{sec:forward}, Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}): a total compilation
    from active trails to graph reachability, certified by node-survival proofs.
  \item We mechanize the reverse witness extractor
    (Section~\ref{sec:reverse}, Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}): every stage is
    constructive, including \lean{route_improves_of_bad}, which strictly
    decreases a well-founded measure.
  \item We close the \textbf{full equivalence}
    (\lean{dSeparated_iff_dSeparates}): on the pairwise-disjoint domain,
    moral-graph separation holds if and only if every trail is blocked, with
    both directions discharged by the optimizer and the decompiler.
  \item We bridge the graph core to a Shannon leakage bound via a
    two-stage factorisation split (Section~\ref{sec:two-stage}): a
    computable upper stage producing \lean{decide}-able Markov-blanket
    conditions, and a typed lower stage delivering the probabilistic
    conditional-independence predicate \lean{condMarkov}.  Composing the
    split with a dual KL witness yields an upper bound for information
    leakage.
\end{enumerate}

The graph results are available as a standalone Lean~4 package.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/OstensibleParadox/CasualQIF}}  The
decompiler produces not a mere existence proof but a computational
witness---a $\Sigma$-type containing the actual trail and the proof that it is
unblocked.

\section{Background and the Endpoint Discrepancy}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Trail Blocking (Operational Semantics)}

An undirected \emph{trail} to $y$ in a DAG $G$ is a finite walk that may
follow edges forward or backward.  A consecutive triple $a \to b \to c$ or
$a \leftarrow b \to c$ is a \emph{non-collider}; $a \to b \leftarrow c$ is a
\emph{collider}.  A triple is \emph{blocked} by $Z$ if:
\begin{itemize}
  \item it is a non-collider whose middle node is in $Z$; or
  \item it is a collider whose middle node and all descendants are disjoint from $Z$.
\end{itemize}
A trail is \emph{blocked} by $Z$ if some internal triple is blocked.  Then $Z$
\emph{d-separates} $X$ from $Y$ in the trail sense if every trail from $X$ to $Y$
is blocked by $Z$.

\subsection{Moral Graph Separation (Static Analysis)}

The \emph{ancestral subgraph} of $S$ is the subgraph induced by all ancestors of
nodes in $S$.  Its \emph{moral graph} is obtained by (1)~forgetting edge
directions and (2)~connecting all co-parents (pairs of nodes with a common child).
The \emph{d-separation graph} is the moral graph of the ancestral subgraph of
$X \cup Y \cup Z$ with $Z$ removed.  Then $Z$ d-separates $X$ from $Y$ in the
graph sense if no node of $X$ is connected to any node of $Y$ in this graph.

\subsection{The Endpoint Discrepancy (Counterexample)}\label{sec:counterexample}

Our counterexample lives on the simplest non-trivial DAG:

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[node distance=1.5cm, every node/.style={circle,draw,minimum size=0.7cm}]
  \node (n0) {$0$};
  \node (n1) [right of=n0] {$1$};
  \node (n2) [right of=n1] {$2$};
  \draw[-{Stealth}] (n0) -- (n1);
  \draw[-{Stealth}] (n1) -- (n2);
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

Let $X = \{0\}$, $Y = \{1\}$, $Z = \{0\}$.\footnote{Node $2$ and the edge $1 \to 2$ represent a downstream path to illustrate a general chain, though the minimal discrepancy itself is exhibited directly on the edge $0 \to 1$.}

\begin{theorem}[Endpoint-in-$Z$ counterexample]\label{thm:counterexample}
There exists a DAG $G$ and finite sets $X, Y, Z \subseteq \N$ such that
$G$ d-separates $X$ from $Y$ in the moral-graph sense, but $Z$ does \emph{not}
d-separate $X$ from $Y$ in the trail-blocking sense.
\end{theorem}

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Mechanized counterexample}]
theorem dsep_complete_endpoint_in_Z_counterexample :
    \exists (G : DAG) (X Y Z : Finset \N),
      DAG.dSeparated G X Y Z \wedge \neg dSeparates G X Y Z := by
  refine <chain3, {0}, {1}, {0}, ?_, ?_>
  ...
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Why the disagreement happens.} Trail blocking only inspects
\emph{internal} triples.  The one-edge trail $0 \to 1$ has no internal triple at
all, so nothing can block it.  By contrast, the moral-graph construction deletes
$Z$ unconditionally; since $0 \in Z$, the edge $(0,1)$ disappears from the
moralized graph, and $0$ and $1$ are disconnected.

\subsection{Textbook Equivalence and the Disjointness Repair}\label{sec:repair}

Standard references state that the two criteria are equivalent under the implicit
assumption that $X$, $Y$, and $Z$ are pairwise disjoint.  In applications,
however, the disjointness precondition is often omitted.  As shown in the
counterexample above (Section~\ref{sec:counterexample}), this omission is not benign.

If we require $X \cap Z = \emptyset$ and
$Y \cap Z = \emptyset$ (enforced by the safety invariant
\lean{DisjointSets X Y Z}), the start node of any trail is never deleted, and the
endpoint caveat disappears.

\section{A Three-Layer Architecture}\label{sec:architecture}

Our formal development uses three layers mirroring a verified compiler's architecture.

\subsection{Layer 1: Type-Safe Graph Syntax}

Nodes are natural numbers, edges are a finite set of ordered pairs
($\mathrm{Finset}\,(\N \times \N)$), and acyclicity is well-foundedness of the
edge relation.  The key safety invariant is
\lean{DisjointSets X Y Z}, which requires pairwise disjointness of the three
node sets.  This constraint can be conceptualized by analogy to substructural types
as an ownership boundary: the conditioned variables $Z$ must be disjoint from
(and thus cannot alias) the source $X$ or sink $Y$.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={DAG structure and safety predicate}]
structure DAG where
  nodes : Finset \N
  edges : Finset (\N \times \N)
  edges_subset : \forall {u v}, (u,v) \in edges -> u \in nodes \wedge v \in nodes
  acyclic : WellFounded fun u v => (u,v) \in edges

def DisjointSets (X Y Z : Finset \N) : Prop :=
  Disjoint X Y \wedge Disjoint X Z \wedge Disjoint Y Z
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Layer 2: Certified Intermediate Representations}

Between operational trails and graph reachability sits a family of IRs that
preserve just enough structure for certified translation.

\paragraph{MAGWalk.} A compressed walk language equivalent to moral-graph
reachability.  It has three constructors: reflexivity, a single directed or
reversed edge (\lean{MAGWalk.single}), and a ``moral jump'' over an active
collider (\lean{MAGWalk.jump}).  The collider node is skipped, which is why
compression is correct.

\paragraph{StaticRoute.} A type-valued decompiler IR that keeps the witness
hidden by graph reachability.  Each step is either a forward edge, a backward
edge, or a moral jump storing the common child:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Static route IR (excerpt)}]
inductive StaticStep (G : DAG) (X Y Z : Finset \N) : \N -> \N -> Type where
  | directForward  (hEdge : G.HasEdge u v) ...
  | directBackward (hEdge : G.HasEdge v u) ...
  | moralJump (huw : G.HasEdge u w) (hvw : G.HasEdge v w)
      (hw : w \in G.ancestralSubgraphNodes (X \cup Y \cup Z)) ...
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{OpenTrace.} The compiler target before converting to an active route.
Each \lean{cons} node stores its oriented DAG traversal \emph{and} a local
non-blocking proof, making \lean{OpenTrace} a type-valued certificate that every
junction is $Z$-open.

\subsection{Two-Stage Factorisation Split and QIF Bridge}\label{sec:two-stage}

The d-separation result supports a Shannon leakage-bound pipeline.
Once the equivalence between moral-graph separation and trail blocking is
established, we can replace a conditional-independence premise by a checked
graph property in a cut-set capacity argument. The full finite chain is:
\[
\text{d-separation} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{conditional independence}
\;\Rightarrow\; \text{conditional DPI} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{leakage bound}.
\]

To define the external QIF interface, we cite the companion audit manuscript
\cite{agentaudit2026}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Upper stage.} Markov-blanket and local-Markov conditions for
    finite DAGs are decidable.  The implementation is in
    \lean{Graph/MarkovBlanket.lean}; the helpers are
    \lean{computeMarkovBlanket}, \lean{generateMarkovConditions}, and
    \lean{generateMarkovBlanketConditions}.  \lean{chain3} and
    \lean{collider3} are discharged by \lean{decide} in
    \lean{Examples/MarkovBlanket.lean}.
  \item \textbf{Lower stage (concrete-tuple adapter).}
    \lean{CausalModel/Factorization.lean} converts those node-set premises into
    \lean{condMarkov} via \lean{condMarkovNodeCI} and the typed 4-variable
    adapter.  \lean{FactorizesOverDAG} captures the semantic joint without
    explicit row/column expansion.
\end{itemize}

The two stages compose: a topological proof obligation discharged by
\lean{decide} flows through the adapter to a probabilistic CI fact usable in
the leakage chain.

\paragraph{Upper-bound certificate.} On top of the split we
prove a sufficiency theorem for leakage bounds via a dual KL witness.
Given a witness distribution $\omega(b\mid c)$,
\lean{condMutualInfo_le_of_dual_witness} bounds the Shannon leakage
$I(A;B\mid C)$ by the KL divergence between the joint and the factorized
witness.  The statement establishes sufficiency for a given witness and does
not claim tightness or an optimizer.



\section{Forward Soundness}\label{sec:forward}

Under $\text{DisjointSets}\,X\,Y\,Z$, we mechanize the soundness direction:
\[
\text{DAG.dSeparated}(G,X,Y,Z) \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{dSeparates}(G,X,Y,Z).
\]

The proof follows a three-stage compilation pipeline.

\paragraph{Stage 1: Trail $\to$ Bayes-ball path.} If a trail $t$ from $x \in X$
to $y \in Y$ is not blocked by $Z$, and $x \notin Z$ (guaranteed by
$\text{Disjoint}\,X\,Z$), then we lift $t$ to an explicit Bayes-ball state path.
The construction is by induction on the trail, analyzing each directional triple
to show that the Bayes-ball state machine can traverse it.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Trail to Bayes-ball path}]
def bayesBallPath_of_active_trail_outOf
    (t : Trail G u v) (h_active : \neg t.isBlocked Z)
    (huZ : u \notin Z) :
    \Sigma final_dir, BayesBallPath G Z (u, outOf) (v, final_dir)
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Stage 2: Certified membership.} The Bayes-ball path contains
intermediate states that must ``survive'' deletion of $Z$.  We prove a certified
version that guarantees every \lean{RequiredState} node belongs to the
d-separation graph nodes:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Certified Bayes-ball path}]
def bayesBallPathCert_of_active_trail_outOf
    (t : Trail G u v) (h_active : \neg t.isBlocked Z)
    (huZ : u \notin Z)
    (huA : u \in G.ancestralSubgraphNodes (X \cup Y \cup Z))
    (hvD : v \in G.dSeparationGraphNodes X Y Z) :
    \Sigma final_dir, {p : BayesBallPath ... //
      \forall {q}, BayesBallPath.RequiredState p q ->
        q.1 \in G.dSeparationGraphNodes X Y Z}
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Stage 3: Compress to MAGWalk.} A Bayes-ball path is a sequence of
single-edge steps.  The \lean{compress} algorithm scans it with a two-step
window: ordinary non-collider windows become a \lean{MAGWalk.single}, while
active-collider windows $(a,\_) \to (b,\text{into}) \to (c,\text{outOf})$ become
a single \lean{MAGWalk.jump} over the collider $b$.  The collider itself need not
survive deletion of $Z$, which is why this compression is correct.

\begin{theorem}[Soundness]\label{thm:soundness}
For any DAG $G$ and pairwise-disjoint finite sets $X, Y, Z \subseteq \N$,
if $G$ d-separates $X$ from $Y$ in the moral-graph sense, then every trail
from $X$ to $Y$ is blocked by $Z$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Top-level soundness theorem}]
theorem dSeparated_of_dSeparated_disjoint
    (hXYZ : DisjointSets X Y Z)
    (hsep : DAG.dSeparated G X Y Z) : dSeparates G X Y Z
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Reverse Witness Extraction}\label{sec:reverse}

The reverse direction reconstructs an active-trail witness from
$\neg\text{DAG.dSeparated}$, showing that the conditioning set $Z$ fails to
block the flow.
The pipeline is:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{Reachable}
&\;\to\; \text{StaticRoute}
\;\to\; \text{NormalizedStaticRoute} \\
&\;\to\; \text{OpenTrace}
\;\to\; \text{ActiveRoute}
\;\to\; \exists\,\text{Trail},\;\neg\text{isBlocked}.
\end{aligned}
\]

\subsection{From Reachability to StaticRoute}

Graph reachability is a \lean{Prop}-valued relation that hides the structure of
each step.  We introduce \lean{StaticStep} and \lean{StaticRoute} as type-valued
evidence: a \lean{StaticRoute} is an explicit list of directed edges, reversed
edges, and moral jumps, each carrying the witness needed for later stages.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Reachability to static route}]
theorem nonemptyStaticRoute_of_dSeparationGraph_reachable
    (h : (G.dSeparationGraph X Y Z).Reachable u v) :
    Nonempty (StaticRoute G X Y Z u v)
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Normalization: Eliminating Bad Colliders}

A ``bad collider'' is a collider window whose middle node and all descendants are
disjoint from $Z$.  Such a collider is blocked, so it must be eliminated.  Normalization may change endpoints: if a bad collider leaks
ancestry into $X$ or $Y$, we reroute the prefix or suffix to a different node in
that set.

We package static routes as endpoint-carrying witnesses
(\lean{StaticRouteWitness}) and define a bad-collider count \lean{routeBadCount}.
The minimization is a \lean{Nat.find} wrapper: if every nonzero-count witness can
be strictly improved, a zero-count witness exists.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Bad-collider minimization wrapper}]
theorem normalized_route_exists_of_improves
    (hwit : \exists w : StaticRouteWitness G X Y Z, True)
    (himprove : \forall w, routeBadCount w \neq 0 ->
      \exists w', routeBadCount w' < routeBadCount w) :
    \exists w, routeBadCount w = 0
\end{lstlisting}

The improvement obligation is discharged by the core normalization theorem,
which has no \lean{sorry}:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Strict improvement for non-normalized routes}]
theorem route_improves_of_bad
    (w : StaticRouteWitness G X Y Z) (hbad : routeBadCount w \neq 0) :
    \exists w' : StaticRouteWitness G X Y Z,
      routeBadCount w' < routeBadCount w
\end{lstlisting}

The dispatcher proof extracts the first bad collider, exhibits an
ancestral escape into $X$ or $Y$, and reattaches a chain whose nodes
all survive in the d-separation graph.  Replacing one moral jump by
zero-cost chain edges strictly decreases the bad-collider count, so the
well-founded recursion bottoms out at zero bad colliders.  Rerouting is
permitted to land on a different node of $X$ or $Y$.\footnote{The proof is structured via several constituent lemmas (\lean{exists_split}, \lean{ancestor_escape}, \lean{bad_child_survives}, \lean{escape_path_survives}, and corresponding count-reduction lemmas) implemented within the decompiler normalization module.}

\subsection{From Zero-Bad StaticRoute to OpenTrace to ActiveRoute}

A static route with zero bad colliders compiles directly to an \lean{OpenTrace},
because every junction is locally open:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Zero-bad compiler}]
theorem openTrace_of_countBadColliders_zero
    (hzero : countBadColliders arrival route = 0) :
    Nonempty (\Sigma finalDir, OpenTrace G Z (x, arrival) (y, finalDir))
\end{lstlisting}

The \lean{OpenTrace} is then converted to a \lean{BayesBallPath} and wrapped as
an \lean{ActiveRoute}.  Finally, \lean{ActiveRoute.to_activeTrail} yields the
existential witness:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Final decompiler theorem}]
theorem activeWitness_of_not_dSeparated
    (hnot : \neg DAG.dSeparated G X Y Z) : ActiveWitness G X Y Z
\end{lstlisting}

The result is a constructive $\Sigma$-type containing the actual nodes $x \in X$,
$y \in Y$, the final direction, and the active route.

\subsection{The Closed Equivalence}

Composing the optimizer (Section~\ref{sec:forward}) and the decompiler yields
the full equivalence on the well-typed domain---the bisimulation
statement: on pairwise-disjoint $X,Y,Z$, the operational and static languages
agree exactly.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Full d-separation equivalence}]
theorem dSeparated_iff_dSeparates
    (hXYZ : DisjointSets X Y Z) :
    DAG.dSeparated G X Y Z <-> dSeparates G X Y Z
\end{lstlisting}

The lower stage takes \lean{FactorizesOverDAG} as a caller-supplied

\end{remark}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Our mechanization bridges causal inference, mechanized probabilistic semantics, and verified compilation.

\paragraph{Mechanized Causal Inference and D-Separation.}
Chancelier, De Lara, and Heymann~(2024) \cite{chancelier2024conditional} mechanized d-separation as a binary relation using Coq and MathComp/SSReflect. Anna Zhang~(2025) \cite{coqci2025} proves the semantic equivalence in Coq with \lean{member u Z = false} and \lean{member v Z = false} already built into the theorem statement, while \lean{CausalQIF}~\cite{causalqif2026} proves the set-wise graph equivalence \lean{dSeparated_iff_dSeparates} under the explicit \lean{disjointSets X Y Z} hypothesis.

Our work differs in two concrete respects.  First, we mechanize a certified witness-extraction pipeline \lean{StaticRoute} $\to$ \lean{OpenTrace} $\to$ \lean{ActiveRoute} that decompiles moral-graph reachability into an explicit active-trail witness, which \lean{CausalQIF} does not provide.  Second, our reverse direction includes \lean{route_improves_of_bad}, a proved bad-collider normalization whose strict decrease on a well-founded measure discharges the termination obligation.  These certificates are then fed into the QIF bridge.

\paragraph{Categorical and Topological D-Separation.}
Fritz and Klingler~(2023) \cite{fritz2023dseparation}, extending work by Fritz and Liang~(2023) \cite{fritz2023free}, abstracted d-separation using string diagrams in Markov categories. Their framework bypasses moralization and reads conditional independence from topological connectedness in a generalized measure-theoretic space. Our approach is complementary: Markov categories provide a general categorical theory, while our artifact targets the legacy operational graph definitions (moral graphs and trace trails) used in existing software. By framing \lean{DisjointSets} as a disjointness constraint, we show that classical d-separation behaves like a disjointness-constrained query syntax within a broader categorical structure.

\paragraph{Trace Bisimulation and Witness Extraction.}
The forward pipeline (soundness) translates active trails into a compressed step-relation (\lean{MAGWalk}), in the spirit of semantics-preserving compiler passes such as CompCert~(2009) \cite{leroy2009formal}. The reverse pipeline (completeness) unwinds reachability into an active trail, resembling Proof-Carrying Code~(1997) \cite{necula1997proof}: instead of returning a boolean assertion of an information leak, it synthesizes an explicit machine-checkable witness. This provides a graph-theoretic substrate for future verified static analyzers of quantitative information flow.



\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

The textbook equivalence of trail blocking and moral-graph separation is
not unconditional: a one-edge trail with an endpoint in $Z$ is enough to
break it.  Mechanizing the repair as a set-theoretic disjointness precondition
(\lean{DisjointSets}) recovers the equivalence in both directions and makes
the disjointness premise explicit.  For verified causal-inference tooling the
consequence is practical: any pipeline that consumes d-separation as a
precondition for conditional independence must surface the disjointness
premise as a checked side condition.

The result covers the structural half of a Shannon
leakage chain whose connection to probability distributions is established
conditional on factorization, marking the interface boundary between
graph topology and measure theory. Future work targets: (1) formalizing
concrete numeric probability mass functions to exercise the verification
chain end-to-end; (2) transitioning from custom graph logic imports to
targeted mathlib theorem structures; (3) lifting the probabilistic foundations
from discrete finite distributions to general measure-theoretic spaces; and
(4) mechanizing the first-order surface syntax, rank checker, and query safety
guarantees.
