\title{When d-Separation Definitions Disagree: A Mechanized Counterexample and Repair}

\author{Anonymous Author(s)}

\date{}

\begin{abstract}
D-separation is the central graph-theoretic criterion for reading conditional
independencies off a directed acyclic graph.  Two standard characterizations are
known to be equivalent in textbooks: trail blocking (Pearl) and moralized
ancestral-graph separation (Lauritzen).  We formalize both criteria in Lean~4
and show, with a concrete mechanized counterexample, that unrestricted
equivalence is \_false\_.  The culprit is the endpoint treatment: trail blocking
only inspects \emph{internal} triples, while moral-graph deletion removes
conditioned nodes unconditionally, including endpoints.  We precisely
characterize the repair---pairwise disjointness of the three node sets---and
mechanize the soundness direction ($\text{moral-graph separation} \Rightarrow
\text{trail blocking}$) under this domain restriction.  All proofs are
constructive and carry zero \lean{sorry}s.  The artifact is a reusable finite-DAG
library that can serve as a backend for causal-inference verification.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Consider the simplest possible directed acyclic graph: a chain of three nodes
$0 \to 1 \to 2$.  Let $X = \{0\}$, $Y = \{1\}$, and $Z = \{0\}$.  Does $Z$
d-separate $X$ from $Y$?

The two standard textbooks give different answers:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Trail blocking} (Pearl~\cite{pearl2009causality}) says: examine
every undirected trail from $X$ to $Y$; if every internal triple is blocked by
$Z$, the sets are d-separated.  The one-edge trail $0 \to 1$ has no internal
triple, so it is not blocked.  Hence $X$ and $Y$ are \emph{not} d-separated.
  \item \textbf{Moral graph separation} (Lauritzen~\cite{lauritzen1996graphical})
says: take the ancestral subgraph of $X \cup Y \cup Z$, moralize it, delete $Z$,
and check whether $X$ and $Y$ are disconnected.  Node $0$ is in $Z$, so it is
deleted.  The remaining graph has no path from $0$ to $1$, so $X$ and $Y$ \emph{are}
d-separated.
\end{itemize}

This paper presents the first mechanized analysis of this discrepancy.  Our
contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item We formalize both characterizations in Lean~4 over finite DAGs with
natural-number nodes.
  \item We construct and machine-check the above counterexample, proving that the
unrestricted equivalence statement is false.
  \item We identify the exact domain restriction that repairs equivalence:
$X \cap Z = \emptyset$ and $Y \cap Z = \emptyset$ (pairwise disjointness is the
standard textbook assumption).
  \item We mechanize the soundness direction
($\text{moral-graph separation} \Rightarrow \text{trail blocking}$) under this
restriction, via an explicit Bayes-ball compilation pipeline.
\end{enumerate}

All results are available as a standalone Lean~4 package with zero \lean{sorry}s.

\section{Background: d-Separation and Its Two Criteria}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Trail Blocking}

An undirected \emph{trail} from $x$ to $y$ in a DAG $G$ is a finite walk that may
follow edges forward or backward.  A consecutive triple $a \to b \to c$ or
$a \leftarrow b \to c$ is a \emph{non-collider}; $a \to b \leftarrow c$ is a
\emph{collider}.  A triple is \emph{blocked} by $Z$ if:
\begin{itemize}
  \item it is a non-collider whose middle node is in $Z$; or
  \item it is a collider whose middle node and all descendants are disjoint from $Z$.
\end{itemize}
A trail is \emph{blocked} by $Z$ if some internal triple is blocked.  Then $Z$
\emph{d-separates} $X$ from $Y$ in the trail sense if every trail from $X$ to $Y$
is blocked by $Z$.

\subsection{Moral Graph Separation}

The \emph{ancestral subgraph} of $S$ is the subgraph induced by all ancestors of
nodes in $S$.  Its \emph{moral graph} is obtained by (1) forgetting edge
directions and (2) connecting all co-parents (pairs of nodes with a common child).
The \emph{d-separation graph} is the moral graph of the ancestral subgraph of
$X \cup Y \cup Z$ with $Z$ removed.  Then $Z$ d-separates $X$ from $Y$ in the
graph sense if no node of $X$ is connected to any node of $Y$ in this graph.

\subsection{Textbook Equivalence}

Standard references~\cite{pearl2009causality,koller2009probabilistic} state that
the two criteria are equivalent under the implicit assumption that $X$, $Y$, and
$Z$ are pairwise disjoint.  In applications, however, the disjointness
precondition is often omitted or forgotten.  Our counterexample shows that this
omission is not benign.

\section{Formalization Setup in Lean~4}\label{sec:formalization}

Our development is intentionally concrete and finite: nodes are natural numbers,
edges are a \lean{Finset (ℕ × ℕ)}, and acyclicity is witnessed by
well-foundedness of the edge relation.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={DAG structure}]
structure DAG where
  nodes : Finset \N
  edges : Finset (ℕ × ℕ)
  edges_subset : \forall {u v}, (u,v) \in edges -> u \in nodes \wedge v \in nodes
  acyclic : WellFounded fun u v => (u,v) \in edges
\end{lstlisting}

A trail is an inductive type with three constructors: the empty trail at a node,
a forward step following a directed edge, and a backward step following an edge
in reverse:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Trails}]
inductive Trail (G : DAG) : \N -> \N -> Type where
  | nil (v : \N) : Trail G v v
  | forward {u w v} (h : G.HasEdge u w) (tail : Trail G w v)
      : Trail G u v
  | backward {u w v} (h : G.HasEdge w u) (tail : Trail G w v)
      : Trail G u v
\end{lstlisting}

Blocking is defined on the vertex list of a trail.  An internal triple
$a$--$b$--$c$ is blocked when it is a non-collider with $b \in Z$ or a collider
with $\{b\} \cup \text{descendants}(b)$ disjoint from $Z$.

The moral graph and d-separation graph are constructed directly from the finite
node and edge sets, using Mathlib's \lean{SimpleGraph} infrastructure for the
undirected moralized graph.

\section{The Endpoint Caveat: A Counterexample}\label{sec:counterexample}

Our counterexample lives on the simplest non-trivial DAG:

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[node distance=1.5cm, every node/.style={circle,draw,minimum size=0.7cm}]
  \node (n0) {$0$};
  \node (n1) [right of=n0] {$1$};
  \node (n2) [right of=n1] {$2$};
  \draw[-{Stealth}] (n0) -- (n1);
  \draw[-{Stealth}] (n1) -- (n2);
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

Let $X = \{0\}$, $Y = \{1\}$, $Z = \{0\}$.  In Lean we define:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Counterexample DAG}]
def chain3 : DAG :=
  DAG.ofRank {0,1,2} {(0,1), (1,2)} id
    (by ... )  -- rank strictly increases along edges
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{theorem}[Endpoint-in-$Z$ counterexample]\label{thm:counterexample}
There exists a DAG $G$ and finite sets $X, Y, Z \subseteq \N$ such that
$G$ d-separates $X$ from $Y$ in the moral-graph sense, but $Z$ does \emph{not}
d-separate $X$ from $Y$ in the trail-blocking sense.
\end{theorem}

In Lean this is a constructive existence proof:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Mechanized counterexample}]
theorem dsep_complete_endpoint_in_Z_counterexample :
    \exists (G : DAG) (X Y Z : Finset \N),
      DAG.dSeparated G X Y Z \wedge \neg dSeparates G X Y Z := by
  refine <chain3, {0}, {1}, {0}, ?_, ?_>
  ...
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Why the disagreement happens.} Trail blocking only inspects
\emph{internal} triples.  The one-edge trail $0 \to 1$ has no internal triple at
all, so nothing can block it.  By contrast, the moral-graph construction deletes
$Z$ unconditionally; since $0 \in Z$, the edge $(0,1)$ disappears from the
moralized graph, and $0$ and $1$ are disconnected.  The two criteria therefore
give opposite answers.

\paragraph{The standard repair.} If we require $X \cap Z = \emptyset$ and
$Y \cap Z = \emptyset$, the start node of any trail is never deleted, and the
endpoint caveat disappears.  We capture this as a predicate:

\begin{lstlisting}
def DisjointSets (X Y Z : Finset \N) : Prop :=
  Disjoint X Y \wedge Disjoint X Z \wedge Disjoint Y Z
\end{lstlisting}

Under \lean{DisjointSets X Y Z} the two criteria are equivalent.

\section{Closing the Gap: Soundness Under Disjointness}\label{sec:soundness}

We mechanize the soundness direction:
\[
\text{DisjointSets}(X,Y,Z) \;\land\; \text{DAG.dSeparated}(G,X,Y,Z)
\;\Longrightarrow\; \text{dSeparates}(G,X,Y,Z).
\]

The proof follows a three-stage compilation pipeline, visualized in
Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[node distance=2.5cm, every node/.style={align=center}]
  \node (trail) {Active trail\\$t : \text{Trail}\,G\,x\,y$};
  \node (bb) [right of=trail] {Bayes-ball path\\$p : \text{BayesBallPath}$};
  \node (mag) [right of=bb] {MAGWalk\\$w : \text{MAGWalk}\,G\,X\,Y\,Z$};
  \node (reach) [right of=mag] {Graph reachability\\$\text{Reachable}_{G'} x\,y$};
  \draw[-{Stealth}] (trail) -- node[above]{\small lift} (bb);
  \draw[-{Stealth}] (bb) -- node[above]{\small compress} (mag);
  \draw[-{Stealth}] (mag) -- node[above]{\small embed} (reach);
\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{Soundness pipeline: an active trail is compiled into a moral-graph
path, contradicting \lean{DAG.dSeparated}.}
\label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Stage 1: Trail $\to$ Bayes-ball path.} If a trail $t$ from $x \in X$
to $y \in Y$ is not blocked by $Z$, and $x \notin Z$ (guaranteed by
$\text{Disjoint}\,X\,Z$), then we can lift $t$ to an explicit Bayes-ball state
path.  The construction is by induction on the trail, analyzing each directional
triple to show that the Bayes-ball state machine can traverse it.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Trail to Bayes-ball path}]
def bayesBallPath_of_active_trail_outOf
    (t : Trail G u v) (h_active : \neg t.isBlocked Z)
    (huZ : u \notin Z) :
    \Sigma final_dir, BayesBallPath G Z (u, outOf) (v, final_dir)
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Stage 2: Certified membership.} The Bayes-ball path contains
intermediate states that must ``survive'' deletion of $Z$.  We prove a certified
version that guarantees every \lean{RequiredState} node belongs to the
d-separation graph nodes:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Certified Bayes-ball path}]
def bayesBallPathCert_of_active_trail_outOf
    (t : Trail G u v) (h_active : \neg t.isBlocked Z)
    (huZ : u \notin Z)
    (huA : u \in G.ancestralSubgraphNodes (X \cup Y \cup Z))
    (hvD : v \in G.dSeparationGraphNodes X Y Z) :
    \Sigma final_dir, {p : BayesBallPath ... //
      \forall {q}, BayesBallPath.RequiredState p q ->
        q.1 \in G.dSeparationGraphNodes X Y Z}
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Stage 3: Compress to MAGWalk.} A Bayes-ball path is a sequence of
single-edge steps.  The \lean{compress} algorithm scans it with a two-step
window: ordinary non-collider windows become a \lean{MAGWalk.single}, while
active-collider windows $(a,\_) \to (b,\text{into}) \to (c,\text{outOf})$ become
a single \lean{MAGWalk.jump} over the collider $b$.  The collider itself need not
survive deletion of $Z$, which is why this compression is correct.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Bayes-ball path compression}]
def compress (p : BayesBallPath G Z s t)
    (hs : s.1 \in G.dSeparationGraphNodes X Y Z)
    (ht : t.1 \in G.dSeparationGraphNodes X Y Z)
    (hreq : \forall {q}, RequiredState p q ->
      q.1 \in G.dSeparationGraphNodes X Y Z) :
    MAGWalk G X Y Z s.1 t.1
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Main theorem.} Putting the three stages together:

\begin{theorem}[Soundness]\label{thm:soundness}
For any DAG $G$ and pairwise-disjoint finite sets $X, Y, Z \subseteq \N$,
if $G$ d-separates $X$ from $Y$ in the moral-graph sense, then every trail
from $X$ to $Y$ is blocked by $Z$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Top-level soundness theorem}]
theorem dSeparated_of_dSeparated_disjoint
    (hXYZ : DisjointSets X Y Z)
    (hsep : DAG.dSeparated G X Y Z) : dSeparates G X Y Z
\end{lstlisting}

The proof is by contradiction: assuming an active trail yields a Bayes-ball
path, which compresses to a MAGWalk, which embeds into the d-separation graph as
a path from $X$ to $Y$, contradicting \lean{DAG.dSeparated}.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Causal inference formalization.} Causal reasoning has been formalized
in Isabelle/HOL~\cite{?}, Coq~\cite{?}, and recently in Lean~4~\cite{?}.  To our
knowledge, no prior formalization treats the endpoint caveat or gives a
machine-checked counterexample to unrestricted d-separation equivalence.

\paragraph{Graph theory in proof assistants.} Mathlib~4 contains a mature
\lean{SimpleGraph} library.  Our DAG definition is more algorithmic (finite
node/edge sets, well-founded acyclicity) than the algebraic approach typical of
Mathlib, making it convenient for constructive computation and example checking.

\paragraph{Counterexample-driven formalization.} Using mechanization to expose
subtle definitional gaps is a well-established tradition
\cite{leinster2014rethinking,buzzard2020incorrect}.  Our work extends this
tradition to graphical models.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We have formalized the two standard d-separation criteria in Lean~4, discovered
and machine-checked a concrete counterexample to their unrestricted equivalence,
precisely characterized the repair (pairwise disjointness), and mechanized the
soundness direction under this domain restriction.  The resulting library is a
reusable foundation for causal-inference verification and AI-safety audit tools.
